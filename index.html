    What is a Foreclosure?

    A foreclosure is when a lender of another entity takes possesion of a home due to non-payment of a mortgage, HOA dues, property taxes, etc.
    A short sale is when a lender takes less than what they are owed as payment in full in an effort to avoid the foreclosure process.
    In a foreclosure, the bank has taken possession of the home. In a short sale, the homeowner still owns the home.

    There are several reasons why a homeowner is not able to make payments to their mortgage, property taxes or HOA dues. The reasons vary for every person, whether a death or illness of an earning spouse; a loss of job; a decrease of income; a divorce; or loss of business, these are all considered hardships. A lender will consider a short sale if a homeowner has a hardship. The lender will consider a short sale rather than go to foreclosure because of the costs incurred to foreclose, the length of time to sell the foreclosed property, and the effect of a foreclosure on their books.

    When a homeowner goes to foreclosure, it is devastating to them emotionally, as well as, financially. A foreclosure greatly impacts a homeowner’s credit score and it may take the homeowner up to 7-10 years before they can buy a home again (this may vary depending on how the lender reports it to the credit bureau).
